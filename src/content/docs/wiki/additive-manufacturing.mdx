**Additive Manufacturing (AM)** is the process of joining
materials to make objects from 3D model data, usually layer
upon layer, as opposed to subtractive manufacturing
methodologies.

*Synonyms:* additive fabrication, additive processes, additive
techniques, additive layer manufacturing, layer manufacturing,
and freeform fabrication

---

### Background
Additive manufacturing is important because it allows engineers to make complex, customized, 
and low-volume parts directly from digital models with less dependence on tooling than 
traditional manufacturing. This makes it especially useful for rapid prototyping, design 
iteration, and geometries that would be difficult to produce through subtractive or 
formative methods.

Additive manufacturing has also seen massive investments over the last decade. Below are the
major initiatives in the US.

U.S. Government Focus on Manufacturing:
- 2010: Manufacturing Enhancement Act

### Processes Overview
For additive manufacturing, the following general process is followed:
- A part is first designed as a 3D digital model.
- The model is sliced into many thin cross-sectional layers that the machine can interpret. 
- The AM system builds the part one layer at a time by selectively placing, joining, or solidifying material in the required locations. 
- After the build is complete, the part may go through post-processing steps such as support removal, curing, machining, or surface
 finishing depending on the process and material.

### Design Considerations

These new AM technologies have led to a shift in the relationship between engineers 
and manufacturers, called concurrent engineering.

**Concurrent Engineering** is s a product-development approach where the design, materials 
selection, manufacturing planning, and testing/validation are developed at the same time, 
rather than one after another, and specifically optimized for the processes.

For this to happen, the following needs to be considered:
- How do product design decisions affect manufacturing?
- How do manufacturing decisions affect product design?

**Design for Manufacturing (DFM)** is a product-development methodology in which a component or 
system is designed so it can be manufactured easily, reliably, and at the lowest practical 
cost using available manufacturing processes.
- The goal is to design a product that can be manufactured easily and economically at a high quality.

Manufacturing processes impose constraints on geometries that can be produced. These constraints 
can be either physical limitations or cost increases.

Below is a list of conventional manufacturing processes and their associated design limitations.

**Subtractive:**
- Turning
    - Removal of material from workpiece via turning of workpiece.
    - Best suited to axisymmetric parts, undercuts, and off-axis features often require additional 
    operations or different processes.
- Milling
    - Removal of material from workpiece via turning of tool.
    - Internal corners, deep narrow pockets, and complex internal features are limited by tool geometry, 
    access, and tool deflection.
- Electrical Discharge Machining (EDM)
    - Erosion of material via electrical discharge.
    - Only works on electrically conductive materials and is generally slower and more costly than conventional
     machining for simpler features.

**Formative:**
- Injection Molding
    - Formation of a part by injecting molten material into a shaped mold cavity.
    - Requires high tooling cost and design constraints such as draft angles, uniform wall thickness, and careful 
    control of shrinkage and part ejection.
- Sand Casting
    - Formation of a part by pouring molten metal into a sand mold and allowing it to solidify.
    - Produces lower dimensional accuracy and surface finish than many other processes, and designs must account for draft,
     shrinkage, and possible internal defects.

**Additive Manufacturing:**

The main advantage of AM compared to the traditional approaches is its high design freedom, which allows it to produce internal channels, 
lattice structures, organic geometries, and customized low-volume parts easier. However, AM also has important limitations, including slower 
production speed, reduced surface finish and dimensional accuracy in some processes, material constraints, 
and the need to account for build orientation, support structures, and post-processing requirements.

### Functional Decomposition

An alternative method of classifying AM processes is functional decomposition. This approach:
- Helps reveal similarities between different processes
- Can be used to help conceive of new AM technologies

The following decomposition is based on the research paper: "[A Functional Classification Framework for the Conceptual Design of Additive Manufacturing Technologies](https://asmedigitalcollection.asme.org/mechanicaldesign/article-abstract/133/12/121002/455846/A-Functional-Classification-Framework-for-the?redirectedFrom=fulltext)"

<figure class="img75-figure">
  <Image src={AM_Decomp} alt="Additive Manufacturing Process Functional Decomposition" />
</figure>

The graph above can be interpreted as a functional map of additive manufacturing
processes. Each row represents a subfunction common to
AM, such as storing material, patterning material or energy, creating the primitive, 
providing material for the next layer, and supporting previous layers, while the entries
across the row show the different working principles that can achieve that function.
A given AM technology is then represented by tracing a path through the matrix, 
selecting one working principle for each subfunction, which allows different processes
to be compared based on their underlying functional structure rather than just their 
machine type or material category.
