When engineers have more than one possible solution, they need a way to compare options without just 
relying on instinct. That is where a trade study comes in. A trade study is a structured method for 
evaluating multiple concepts against the criteria that matter most for a project. Instead of asking: 
*Which idea do we like best?*, a trade study asks: *Which idea best fits our goals, constraints, and 
priorities?*

Trade studies are common in systems engineering because most projects involve competing factors. A 
design might be lightweight but expensive, simple but less efficient, or high-performing but difficult 
to manufacture. A trade study helps make those tradeoffs visible.

---

## What a Trade Study Does

At a high level, a trade study helps:

- compare multiple design options
- define evaluation criteria clearly
- assign importance to those criteria
- score each concept in a consistent way
- justify why one option was selected over another

This is useful because engineering decisions usually involve compromise. Very rarely is one concept the 
best in every category.

## Basic Process

A trade study does not need to be overly complicated. A simple process is usually enough.

### 1. Define the decision

Start by being specific about what you are trying to choose. Are you selecting a material, mechanism, 
architecture, process, or vendor? The decision should be narrow enough that the concepts can be compared 
directly.

### 2. List the concepts

Identify the design options you want to compare. These should be realistic alternatives, not random ideas 
with no chance of being used.

### 3. Choose the criteria

Decide what matters for the project. Common criteria include:

- cost
- mass
- power
- reliability
- manufacturability
- schedule
- safety
- complexity
- maintainability
- performance

The criteria should reflect the real goals of the project, not just what is easy to score.

### 4. Assign weights

Not every criterion matters equally. If safety is more important than cost, it should have a larger weight. 
Weights help reflect project priorities.

Some teams use percentages that add to 100. Others use a simple scale such as 1 to 5.

### 5. Score each concept

Score each concept against each criterion using a consistent scale. For example, you might use 1 to 5, where:

- 1 = poor
- 3 = acceptable
- 5 = excellent

Try to base scores on evidence whenever possible, such as calculations, prior testing, vendor data, or 
engineering judgment.

### 6. Calculate totals

Multiply each score by its weight, then add the results for each concept. This gives an overall comparison.

This does not mean the highest number always wins automatically, but it gives a structured basis for discussion.

### 7. Check the result

After calculating totals, step back and ask whether the answer makes sense. If the result seems surprising, 
the team should review the weights, assumptions, and scoring logic. Sometimes the value of the trade study is 
not just the final ranking, but the discussion it creates.

## Common Formats

One of the most common trade study formats is a **decision matrix**, sometimes called a **weighted decision 
matrix**. Another well-known version is the **Pugh matrix**, where concepts are compared relative to a baseline 
or datum. NASA also frames this kind of work more broadly under **decision analysis** within systems engineering. 

A weighted decision matrix is often easier for blog posts, reports, and student projects because it is 
straightforward to explain and calculate.
