### Overview

**Description:** A vat of liquid photopolymer resin is cured through selective exposure to light (via a laser
or projector) which then initiates polymerization and converts the exposed areas to a solid part.

**Alternative Names:**
- SLA™- Stereolithography Apparatus
- DLP™- Digital Light Processing
- 3SP™- Scan, Spin, and Selectively Photocure
- CLIP™ – Continuous Liquid Interface Production

**Strengths:**
- High level of accuracy and complexity
- Smooth surface finish
- Accommodates large build areas

**Materials:**
- UV-Curable Photopolymer Resins

### Vat Photopolymerization

**Vat Photopolymerization (VP)** is an additive manufacturing process in which 
liquid photopolymer resin is selectively cured by exposure to light, typically 
UV light, to build a part layer by layer. In this process family, the resin 
undergoes a chemical reaction in which a photoinitiator and monomer form polymer 
chains and a cross-linked solid structure.

For vat photopolymerization, the following general process is followed:
- A vat is filled with liquid photopolymer resin.
- A UV laser or projected light pattern selectively cures the resin at the 
  surface of the current layer.
- The build platform moves by one layer thickness to allow uncured resin to 
  cover the next layer.
- In top-down systems, the platform lowers into the vat and a recoating blade 
  spreads fresh resin.
- In bottom-up systems, the platform raises and resin flows between the 
  transparent window and the previously cured layer.
- This process repeats until the full geometry is built.
- After printing, the part is cleaned and typically requires UV and/or thermal 
  post-curing.

Vat photopolymerization can be divided into different system approaches, 
including:
- **Top-down VP**
- **Bottom-up VP**
- **Mask Projection Stereolithography**
- **Continuous Liquid Interface Production (CLIP)**

Mask projection stereolithography uses selective UV exposure of an entire layer 
through a digital micromirror display (DMD), where illuminated regions are cured 
and dark regions remain uncured liquid resin. This approach can enable very fine 
feature resolution, including micro-scale features.

Photopolymerization-based AM processes offer several important advantages:
- High feature resolution
- High accuracy
- Excellent surface finish
- Small layer thicknesses
- Potential for high throughput in some systems
- Compatibility with specialized applications such as functionally graded 
  materials in related photopolymer processes

However, vat photopolymerization also has important limitations:
- Support structures are often required for overhangs
- Supports must typically be removed manually
- Parts require post-curing
- Uncured resin can be hazardous to handle
- Printed materials may be brittle
- Parts can be sensitive to heat, humidity, and sunlight
- Materials can be relatively expensive compared to more common thermoplastics

Common vat photopolymerization materials include acrylate- and epoxy-based 
resins, often formulated as combinations to balance cure speed and final 
material properties. These systems are generally thermoset-based rather than 
thermoplastic.


Photopolymerization is a chemical reaction
- Photoinitatior + monomer

Results in polymer chains and cross-linked structure
- In contrast, thermoplastic polymers (e.g., ABS, polystyrene), do not react when
processed via phase change
